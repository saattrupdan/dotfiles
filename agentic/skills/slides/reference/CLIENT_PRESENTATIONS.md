# Client Presentations

How to structure a presentation when the audience is paying for the work, or deciding whether to.

The room is serious but not technical. The goal is concrete: win the work, renew the work, or show what the work bought them. Either way, the audience is asking the same question — *was this (or will this be) worth our money?*

---

## What the audience actually wants

Strip everything else away and a client presentation answers four questions:

1. **Do you understand our problem?**
2. **Is your approach credible?**
3. **What do we get, and when?**
4. **What does it cost, and what's the risk?**

If a slide does not move one of those four questions forward, cut it. Politely, but cut it.

The audience is rarely hostile, but they are *busy*. They are evaluating you against their last meeting, their next meeting, and the vendor they spoke to on Tuesday. Respect their time and they will respect yours.

---

## Tone

Serious, warm, plain-spoken. Not stiff. Not salesy. Not chummy.

You are a professional explaining work to other professionals. The language should be that of a confident specialist talking to an intelligent non-specialist:

- No jargon without a one-line definition.
- No internal codenames. The audience does not know what "Project Falcon" is.
- No hedging that signals lack of conviction ("we sort of think maybe…").
- No promises that signal lack of judgement ("this will definitely solve everything").

A useful target: write every line so it could appear, unchanged, in a follow-up email to the client's CFO.

---

## Two formats, same bones

Client presentations come in two main shapes. They share most of the structure; the emphasis shifts.

### The pitch (selling the work)

You are asking them to spend money. The deck has to earn that ask.

| Section | ~Share | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Opening | 5% | Acknowledge the room and the reason for the meeting |
| Their situation | 15% | Show you understand the problem |
| Our approach | 20% | Explain how you would solve it |
| Evidence | 25% | Show this is not your first time |
| What you get | 15% | Concrete deliverables, timeline, team |
| Investment & risk | 10% | Price, terms, what could go wrong |
| Next step | 10% | One clear, low-friction ask |

### The results readout (showing what was delivered)

You are reporting on money already spent. The deck has to justify it and set up the next conversation.

| Section | ~Share | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Opening | 5% | Recap scope and goals as originally agreed |
| What we did | 15% | The work, summarised |
| What changed | 35% | The outcomes, with numbers |
| What we learned | 15% | Honest read on what worked and what didn't |
| What's next | 20% | Recommended follow-on work |
| Q&A | 10% | Hold space for it |

## Section guidance

### Opening

State the purpose of the meeting in one sentence. State the agenda in one slide. Introduce the people in the room only if the client doesn't already know them, and keep it to one line each: name, role, what they're here to speak to.

Do not begin with your company's history. Do not begin with a slide of your logo and the word "Welcome". Begin with *their* situation.

> *"You asked us to look at why the onboarding funnel was dropping 40% of users in week one. Today we'll walk through what we found, what we changed, and what the numbers look like now."*

### Their situation (or: recap of scope)

This is where you prove you listened.

For a pitch, restate the client's problem in their own words — or close to them. If you've had discovery calls, quote phrases that came up. Name the constraints they're operating under (budget, timeline, internal politics, regulatory). Show that you understand the shape of the thing, not just the headline.

For a readout, restate the scope and goals as agreed at kick-off. This anchors everything that follows against the bar that was set.

> *"At kick-off we agreed on three goals: reduce week-one drop-off below 25%, do it without a redesign, and ship before the May campaign. Here's where we landed against each."*

### Our approach (pitch) / What we did (readout)

The non-technical audience does not need to know the architecture. They need to know:

- The shape of the work (research, build, ship, measure).
- Who does what (your team, their team, any third parties).
- How long each phase takes.
- What they will see from you, and when.

A simple phase diagram works well here. Three to five phases, with the deliverable at the end of each. Avoid Gantt charts unless someone asked.

If the audience has a technical person in the room, prepare one or two backup slides with more depth, but do not lead with them. Bring them out if asked.

### Evidence (pitch) / What changed (readout)

This is the section that wins or loses the room.

**For a pitch:** show two or three case studies. Same shape every time:

- Who the client was (sector, size — anonymise if needed).
- What they came to you with.
- What you did.
- What changed, with a number.

Three case studies beats ten. The audience is pattern-matching to themselves; pick the ones that look most like *them*.

**For a readout:** show the outcomes against the goals agreed at kick-off.

- One headline number per goal, with the baseline next to it.
- Where you have it, a chart that shows the change over the engagement.
- A short narrative line that explains the *why*, not just the *what*.

> *"Week-one drop-off fell from 41% to 22%. The biggest single contributor was the email re-send on day three — that change alone recovered roughly 8 percentage points."*

Honesty matters here. If a metric didn't move, say so on the same slide as the metrics that did. The client already knows; pretending otherwise costs you the trust you built with the wins.

### What you get (pitch) / What we learned (readout)

**For a pitch:** spell out the deliverables. Not aspirations, deliverables. A document, a working system, a trained team, a report, a dashboard. With dates. With acceptance criteria where they exist.

Include the team. The client is partly buying the people. Name them, list relevant experience in one line each, and say who will be their day-to-day contact.

**For a readout:** the honest part of the deck. What surprised you, what you'd do differently, where the work opened up new questions. Two or three points, plainly stated.

This is the section most readouts skip, and it's the one that earns the next contract. Clients can tell when they're being managed. They respond well to being told the truth.

### Investment & risk (pitch) / What's next (readout)

**For a pitch:** the price slide. Don't bury it. Don't apologise for it. Present it the way a confident professional presents a number — clearly, with the structure (fixed fee, T&M, retainer, phased) and the payment terms. If there are options, present two or three, not seven.

Pair it with a short risk slide: the two or three things that could go wrong and how you'd handle each. Naming risk before the client does is one of the strongest trust signals you can send.

**For a readout:** the recommendation for follow-on work, framed in their language. What outcome would the next phase target, how long would it take, what would it cost, and what would you need from them to start. This is a pitch slide in disguise — treat it like one.

### Next step / Q&A

End with a single, specific, low-friction ask.

> *"If this looks right, the next step is a two-hour working session with your data team to agree the metrics. We have three slots open next week."*

> *"Happy to take questions now, and we'll send the deck and the full numbers by end of day."*

Not "let us know what you think." That is not a next step. That is a vibe.

## Slide conventions

### Headlines

Use the headline to state the takeaway of the slide. The audience reads the headline first and decides whether to read the rest.

| Avoid | Prefer |
|-------|--------|
| "Results" | "Week-one drop-off fell from 41% to 22%" |
| "Approach" | "Three phases, ten weeks, fixed fee" |
| "Case study: Acme" | "Acme cut support tickets by 30% in six weeks" |

### Numbers

Big. Single. Anchored.

One number on a slide, in a large font, with the baseline next to it in a smaller font, and a one-line explanation underneath. The audience remembers numbers that stand alone; they forget numbers buried in a table of twelve.

For tables, bold the row that supports the headline. Always include the baseline or the comparison.

### Charts

- Label axes. Include units. Include the time range.
- Annotate the moment the change was introduced.
- Strip everything that doesn't carry information — gridlines, drop shadows, 3D effects.
- If a chart needs more than ten seconds to read, it's too complex for the room.

### Language

- Short sentences. The audience is reading and listening at once.
- Active voice where the agent matters ("We rebuilt the checkout flow") and passive where it doesn't ("Drop-off was measured weekly").
- Spell out acronyms the first time, even ones you think are obvious.
- Currency, percentages, and dates: be consistent throughout the deck.

### Branding

Use the client's terminology where you can. If they call it "customers", don't switch to "users". If they call it "ARR", don't switch to "annual revenue". Mirroring the vocabulary signals that you've absorbed the business.

A light cobrand on the title and closing slides is fine and often welcomed. Do not put both logos on every slide — it looks anxious.

## Handling the room

Client meetings often have a mixed audience: a senior decision-maker, a domain expert, and one or two technical people. Each of them is listening for different things.

- **The decision-maker** wants outcomes, cost, and risk. They will read the headlines and skip the detail.
- **The domain expert** wants to know you understand their world. They will pick up on terminology and on whether your case studies look like their problem.
- **The technical person** wants to know the approach is sound. They will ask the hard questions, and they are usually your strongest ally if you answer them straight.

A good deck lets each of them get what they need without slowing the others down. The headline carries the decision-maker. The body carries the domain expert. The backup slides and the Q&A carry the technical person.

### Questions

- Repeat the question if the room is large or if the question was unclear. It confirms you heard it and gives you a beat to think.
- Answer in one or two sentences first, then expand only if invited.
- If you don't know, say so. Commit to following up by a specific date.
- If a question reveals a misunderstanding, don't defend — clarify. "That's a fair read of the slide. What we actually did was…"

Never argue with the most senior person in the room. If they're wrong about something material, acknowledge their concern, state the data, and offer to walk through it offline.

## Common faults

1. **Leading with your company.** The client did not come to hear about you. They came to hear about themselves.
2. **Generic case studies.** "We worked with a Fortune 500 retailer" is not a case study. Name the sector, the problem, and the number that moved.
3. **Burying the price.** Putting the cost on slide 47 makes it feel like a confession. Put it where it belongs and own it.
4. **Hedging on results.** "Drop-off may have decreased somewhat" is worse than no claim at all. If you have the number, state it. If you don't, say what you have.
5. **No clear ask.** A pitch without a next step is not a pitch. A readout without a follow-on recommendation is a missed renewal.
6. **Death by deliverables list.** Listing 40 line items signals effort, not value. Group them, summarise, and put the detail in an appendix.
7. **Mismatched audience.** A technical slide deck delivered to a board, or a strategy deck delivered to engineers, is the most common reason these meetings go cold. Confirm the audience before you build the deck.

## Length and pacing

The room's attention is a resource. Budget it.

| Meeting length | Slides (rough) | Time on Q&A |
|---------------|----------------|-------------|
| 30 min | 8–12 | 10 min |
| 60 min | 15–20 | 15–20 min |
| 90 min | 20–30 | 25–30 min |

Always finish at least five minutes before the scheduled end. The most important parts of a client meeting — the questions, the side conversations, the "and one more thing" — happen at the end. Don't crowd them out.

---

## A checklist before the meeting

- The client's name and logo are spelt correctly on every slide they appear on.
- The first slide answers "what is this meeting about?" in one sentence.
- Every results slide has a baseline, a number, and a unit.
- Every claim about prior work has a name, a sector, and a number.
- The price slide (or the recommendation slide) has a clear next step beside it.
- The deck reads in order without the speaker — a busy executive will skim it later.
- Backup slides exist for the two or three questions you most expect.
- The deck has been read out loud, end to end, by someone who is not on the project.

---

## Closing note

The client is not buying a deck. They are buying confidence that you will deliver, or evidence that you already have. Every slide either contributes to that or distracts from it.

Be plain. Be specific. Show the numbers. Name the risks. Ask for the next step.

If you do those five things, the deck almost builds itself.
